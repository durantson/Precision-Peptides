Molecular Glossary

Defining the nomenclature and analytical standards of synthetic peptide chemistry.

Acetate Salt

The most common counter-ion used in peptide synthesis to stabilize the molecule. Acetate salts are generally preferred in cell culture research over TFA (Trifluoroacetate) salts due to their lower cytotoxicity.

Amidation (C-Terminus)

A modification where the C-terminal carboxyl group is replaced with an amide group. This mimics the natural state of many hormones and increases the peptide's resistance to carboxypeptidase degradation.

Bacteriostatic Water

Sterile water containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol, used as a preservative to inhibit the growth of bacteria. Essential for the reconstitution of multi-dose research vials.

HPLC

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography. The analytical "gold standard" used to determine the chemical purity of a peptide. It separates the target peptide from impurities based on their interaction with a stationary phase and a mobile phase.

Lyophilization

The process of freeze-drying a peptide to remove solvent. This results in a stable "cake" or powder that can be stored and transported at room temperature without immediate degradation.

Mass Spectrometry (MS)

An analytical technique that measures the mass-to-charge ratio of ions. It is used to verify the exact molecular weight of a synthesized peptide, ensuring the sequence of amino acids is correct.

Reconstitution

The process of adding a liquid solvent (diluent) to a lyophilized (powdered) reagent to return it to a liquid state for research application.

SPPS

Solid Phase Peptide Synthesis. A method of synthesizing peptides by adding amino acids step-by-step to a solid resin support. Invented by Robert Bruce Merrifield, it allows for the precise creation of complex sequences.
